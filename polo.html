Polo: History, Overview, and Rules

Introduction

Polo is an ancient, highly polished team sport that was founded over 2,000 years ago in ancient Persia. Because it requires highly developed athletic skills and horsemanship in almost equal measure, it has acquired the nickname "the sport of kings." Players on horseback try to score goals by hitting a ball with a long-handled mallet into the opponent's goal. Polo has grown to be one of the most favored sports in almost all parts of the world. The origin of this article goes back to tracing where polo is, goes over what polo is, and describes rules and regulations, making it one of the most interesting and thrilling sports in the world.

History of Polo

Its origins are more than 2,500 years old, as it originated in Persia-now Iran-as a training game for cavalry units sometime in the 6th century BC. The game was called "chogan" in Persian and was used to train the soldiers in order to improve their horsemanship; the game was one way through which their military tactics could be devised. By this time, it spread over the entire and was popular amongst Indian, Chinese, and Middle Eastern royalties.

It originated in the Indian subcontinent during the 13th century and rapidly gained popularity amongst the aristocracy and British troops stationed there. It was formalized during the 19th century by British colonial officers who exported it to England and turned it into a widely known sport in no time.

The first official polo club, the Calcutta Polo Club, was founded in 1862, and it wasn't long before England saw its first clubs and tournaments. The United States followed with the United States Polo Association (USPA) in 1890 and still today, the governing body for the sport in the U.S. Today, the game is both professionally and recreationally played throughout much of the world, mainly in countries such as Argentina, the U.S., the U.K., and Australia.

Overview of Polo

Polo is one of the few team sports played quite rapidly on horseback. It deals with a team of four players attempting to hit a small-sized ball into the opponent team's goal. The sport is usually played on a huge open field measuring 300 yards by 160 yards, nearly the size of nine football fields.

Of course, polo also requires speed, coordination, and skill since the horses have to be maneuvered with one hand while the player wields a long mallet with the other. There has to be a mixture of strategy and precision in the field in the way fast plays are made or in the hopes of outplaying the opponent.

Polo Equipment

  1. Polo Pony

    2. Despite the name, these horses are full-sized, often thoroughbreds or Argentine breeds, known for agility and speed. Each player needs several horses, as each "chukka" or period uses fresh horses.

  2. Mallet

    3. The mallet has a long handle of bamboo or composite with a hardwood head. The players hi the ball with the mallet downfield or toward the goal.

  3. Helmet

    4. Considering how dangerous the game is, polo players wear helmets-out of which many have face guards or visors for protection against falls or hits by the mallet or the ball.

  4. Ball

    5. The ball is usually of hard plastic and normally 3 to 3.5 inches in diameter for field polo. For arena polo, it would be slightly larger and softer.

  5. Other Equipment

    6. These include knee guards, gloves, and at times face guards to further protect the player. Riding boots and breeches bring comfort and safety when riding a horse.

Rules and Regulations of Polo

Polo has rules instituted by governing organizations such as the Hurlingham Polo Association (HPA) in the U.K. and the USPA in the U.S. that ensure fair play, safety for the players, and sportsmanship.

Basic Rules of Play

  1. Objective of the Game

    2. To win a game in polo, a team has to score more goals than its opponent. A goal is said to be scored when a player successfully hits the ball between the opponent's goalposts.

  2. Teams and Position

    3. A team is constituted with four players, each number denoting a different position ranging from 1 to 4.

    • Player 1: This usually includes the major offensive player who scores most of the points.
    • Player 2: May be an all-round player, majored in both offense and defense; sometimes used as a backup for Player 1.
    • Player 3: He usually is the captain of the team; they are used in implementing strategies during play to ensure that the pace of the game is controlled.
    • Player 4: This player stays a defensive player who mainly covers his team's goal area.
  3. Periods of Play (Chukkas)

    4. A typical outdoor match is played for 4 to 8 chukkas, each played for 7 minutes. Within this time players change horses so their horses are always fresh.

    At the end of each chukka, the clock stops and only restarts after a short break.

  4. Scoring and Direction of Play

    5. Teams alternate goal direction with each scored goal so that neither team shall have an advantage over the other due to wind direction or field conditions.

  5. Start of Game

    6. The game starts with what is called a "throw-in," where the umpire throws the ball between the two teams lined up opposite each other.

Rules of Movement and Play

  1. Right of Way and Line of the Ball

    2. Polo emphasizes players' safety wherein at every impact of the ball, an imaginary "line of the ball" should be drawn in the direction where the ball moved. Players having the line of the ball are entitled with right of way and opponents have to avoid at all costs crossing this line to prevent collision.

    The right-of-way rule prevents hazardous moves because players continue to move in one direction with less-sharp changes of movement.

  2. Hooking

    3. One player may interfere with another player's swing by "hooking" the opponent's mallet with his own. This hooking can be done only when both players are on one side of the ball, to prevent a hazardous reach across.

  3. Riding Off

    4. Players may take their horses and "ride off" another player's horse to clear that opponent out and away from the ball. Riding off must be shoulder-to-shoulder and at a safe angle so as not to harm horse or player.

Fouls and Penalties

  1. Crossing the Line

    2. A foul is considered when the line of a ball is crossed by a player and the right of way for another player is interfered with.

  2. Hook Incorrectly

    3. If the hooking player is at an angle to the opposing player or crosses over the opposing player's horse, then that will be considered a foul.

  3. Dangerous Riding

    4. A penalty is awarded to avoid injury to horses and players: dangerous riding, for example, bumping at an unsafe angle.

  4. Types of Penalty Shots

    5. Depending on the severity of the foul, penalty shots are awarded at the following distances from the goal:

    • Penalty 1: This is an automatic goal given for serious fouls near the goal.
    • Penalty 2: Free shot from 30 yards out, all other players distance away.
    • Penalty 3: Free kick taken at 40 yards out.
    • Penalty 4: Free kick from 60 yards out.
    • Penalty 5: It provides for a free hit from the point of the foul or from the center, whichever the foul warrants.

Polo Variants

  • Arena Polo

    • Indoor or outdoor arena polo is played on a smaller field with only three players per team, using a larger ball and arena walls to keep the ball constantly in play.

  • Snow Polo

    • The game played on snow fields and generally during winter at some exclusive winter resorts like St. Moritz in Switzerland; horses wear special horseshoes for better grip on the icy grounds.

  • Beach Polo

    • The game played on sand, with generally relaxed rules and more of a promotional attraction in tropical and beach locations.

Conclusion

Polo is an exciting, spellbinding game involving horsemanship, coordination, and strategy on all sides. From its very ancient roots to the internationally renowned prestige of today, polo has been an elegant game combined with aggressiveness. Unique in the rules, such as the right-of-way concept forged with safety paramount in mind, and difficult-to-master equestrian and athletic blends make polo unique in every way from any other sport. Today, polo continues to siphon fans worldwide for protection of a timeless tradition and the generation of inspiration in new generations.
